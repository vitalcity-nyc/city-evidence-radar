How this works

Each weekly run pulls the newest items from the sources above, then an artificial intelligence (AI) model reads the title and abstract of each and rates it on the measures you can see on every card: the size of the demonstrated effect, causal strength (is the evidence experimental or quasi-experimental?), scalability, cost-effectiveness, New York City applicability, how underused the idea is and how fresh its angle is. The overall score, from 0 to 100, weights effect size and rigor most heavily, treats underuse and novelty as secondary and adds a small lift for public-safety ideas. A rigorously evaluated study that found no effect ranks below one that worked.

The default view shows only studies rated as having causal evidence: randomized or strong quasi-experimental designs. Turn that filter off to see the full field, including descriptive and observational work.

About the score

Scores are a triage aid, not a verdict. They are generated from abstracts only, not full papers, so they can misjudge a study's true design, cost or relevance. Always read the linked paper before acting on or publishing anything. The causal-strength label is a heuristic, and a paper's real-world cost depends heavily on local context.

⚠ A note on artificial intelligence in this tool

This radar uses an artificial intelligence (AI) model to triage research. Please keep in mind:

  • Every summary, score, method note and causal-strength label is generated by AI from the abstract only, not the full paper. Treat them as a starting point, not ground truth.
  • The model can misread findings, overstate or understate cost and scalability or misclassify a study's research design.
  • Every rating — effect size, scalability, cost, New York City applicability, how underused or fresh an idea is, and which theme it belongs to — is a judgment call the model makes. Reasonable people, and the authors, may disagree, especially on whether an idea is truly underused.
  • Coverage is partial: the Social Science Research Network (SSRN) and several think tanks block automated access and are not included (see footer).
  • Always open and read the linked study before citing, publishing or acting on any idea here.

How to read this page

Every week the radar pulls the newest research from a set of credible sources, then rates each study for how strong its evidence is and how useful the idea could be to a city. Here is what the controls and labels mean.

Causal evidence only — on by default

Shows only studies whose design can establish cause and effect: randomized controlled trials and strong quasi-experimental designs, such as difference-in-differences, regression discontinuity or natural experiments. Turn it off to also see observational and descriptive studies, which can suggest but not prove that an idea works.

Public safety only

Limits the list to ideas about crime, violence, policing, justice or emergency response. It is off by default, so housing, transit, health, education and other topics also appear.

Search and sort

Search matches the title, authors, the one-line idea, the summary, topic, journal and the two facts on each card. Sort reorders the list by overall score, by date or by a single rating.

The score — from 0 to 100

A triage aid, not a verdict. The size of the demonstrated effect counts most, so a rigorously run study that found no effect ranks below one that moved the needle. Research design, scalability, cost and readiness for New York also weigh heavily, and how underused or fresh an idea is gives it a secondary lift. Public-safety ideas get a small added weight.

The six ratings — each from 1 to 5

Effect and promise is how large and robust the main finding is; a null result scores low. Scalability is how readily the idea could grow citywide. Cost-effectiveness is how cheap it is per person helped. Readiness for New York is how directly the city could adopt it. Underused is the gap between how strong the evidence is and how little the idea is actually tried or funded, so a well-evidenced but rarely used idea scores high while a mainstream one scores low. Fresh angle is how new the way of thinking is.

Size of effect and people reached

Two facts pulled from each abstract: the magnitude and direction of the main finding, and how many people or places the study covered. When an abstract does not give a number, the card says so rather than guessing.

Themes and grouping

Each idea is sorted into one editorial theme, such as reentry, domestic violence or schools. Turn on "Group by theme" to read the ideas as bundles, each with a short framing, rather than as a single ranked list. "Hide null results" drops studies that found no meaningful effect.

The evidence tiers

A randomized controlled trial assigns people or places to a program or a comparison group at random, the strongest test of cause and effect. A strong quasi-experimental design mimics that comparison using real-world variation, and a rigorous systematic review pools many such studies. Observational and descriptive work, shown only when the causal filter is off, can point to promising ideas but cannot prove they cause the result.
